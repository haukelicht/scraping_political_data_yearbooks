Introduction

The year 2014 was marked by the Socialist People's Party leaving the government, elections to the European Parliament, a referendum on the European Patent Court and three party leadership changes. With a general election to be held by September 2015 at the latest, the government was struggling to deliver results that could turn its support.

Election report

On 25 May, Denmark held elections to the EP and, concurrently, a referendum on the European Patent Court.

European Parliament elections, May 2014

The most important feature of the context of Danish EP elections is the degree of opposition to European integration and the EU across the political spectrum. This is most markedly voiced by the left‐wing and right‐wing parties, together with the People's Movement against EU. Even though policy issues made it onto the agenda, the major political issue during the campaign concerned Liberal Party chair and former Prime Minister Lars Løkke Rasmussen, who had gotten clothing worth €20,000 paid for by the party.

As elsewhere in Europe, the forces opposing European integration triumphed. The main winner was the Danish People's Party, which became the largest party for the first time in an election. Its frontrunner, Morten Messerschmidt, got one in five of the valid votes and three out of four votes for the Danish People's Party. The EP election demonstrates the transformation of the Danish People's Party from protest to mainstream, something that is expected to have electoral consequences at the parliamentary elections in 2015.

Among the remaining results, other patterns of interest include that the People's Movement against EU retained its seat; the Social Liberals regained their representation after a period with none; the Conservatives held on to their seat even though electorally challenged; and the Liberals lost, which was attributed to the bad publicity surrounding Løkke.

Referendum on the European Patent Court

Denmark's partaking in the European Patent Court would imply the surrender of sovereignty according to section 20 of the constitution, which implies that the proposal either needs the support of a five‐sixths majority in parliament or a majority of voters at a referendum, who make up at least 30 per cent of the electorate. The proposal did not get the required large majority in a parliamentary vote on 29 April 2014. The public debate prior to the referendum was limited. Two‐thirds of voters supported the proposal, which is a very clear‐cut result compared to previous Danish referendums on EU issues. On this basis, Denmark was able to ratify the intergovernmental agreement on the European Patent Court.

Cabinet report

Thorning‐Schmidt I, ending February 2014

On the ideological composition and other features of Helle Thorning‐Schmidt I, please see Bille (2012, 2013, 2014).

Thorning‐Schmidt II, beginning February 2014

The political compromises within the government became too much for the Socialist People's Party, and on 30 January they left the government. Without any drama, Helle Thorning‐Schmidt formed her second government on 3 February. The Socialist People's Party joined the Red‐Green Alliance as the new government's parliamentary base of support.

The ideological span of the government is reduced on the traditional economic dimension as the party composition is changed. This also goes for the new politics dimension, but to a lesser extent. The number of ministries was reduced from 22 to 20, and some reshuffling of areas took place (see Table 4). In regard to the appointments, the list of ministers became more diverse with both a minister not of ‘ethnic Danish descent’ (Manu Sareen) and a homosexual minister (Mogens Jensen).

Two reshuffles were necessary during 2014. The first was necessitated on 30 August when it became clear that Minister for Economic Affairs and the Interior Margrethe Vestager was to replace Connie Hedegaard as EU Commissioner. When Morten Østergaard was selected as political leader of the Social Liberals (see below), he was appointed as Minister for Economic Affairs and the Interior on 2 September. Østergaard was replaced by Benny Engelbrecht, a rank‐and‐file Social Democratic MP, thus shifting the party balance within the government slightly as a minor compensation for the Social Liberals getting the EU Commissioner position. The second reshuffle was necessary when Minister of Justice Karen Hækkerup resigned for a position as director of an interest organisation. She was replaced by Mette Frederiksen, whose disappointment with the reshuffle was quite clear. Henrik Dam Kristensen, a former minister and experienced politician, took over from Mette Frederiksen in the Ministry of Employment.

Institutional changes

The year 2014 was characterised by three, almost four, party leadership changes that all may have electoral consequences.

New party leader: The Socialist People's Party

Participation in government was a strain on the Socialist People's Party, both because it limited internal party democracy and because the policy concessions of the party were too large for many members who found that the Social Liberals had too much of a say on government policies (see Bille 2013, 2014). The straw that broke the camel's back was the government's decision to sell part of DONG Energy to Goldman Sachs, which created a deep split within the party and parliamentary group. As a consequence, Annette Vilhelmsen withdrew the Socialist People's Party from government and resigned as party chair on 30 January. The two previous party leadership elections in 2005 and 2012 were decided by postal ballots of party members (see Bille 2013: 59, 2006). However, in 2014, Pia Olsen Dyhr was the only candidate, and was therefore appointed without further ado.

New party leader: The Social Liberals

Margrethe Vestager resigned as political leader of the Social Liberals due to her appointment as EU Commissioner. The parliamentary group decided to replace her with Morten Østergaard, who was the natural heir apparent. Within the Social Liberals, the parliamentary group decides on the political leader, who is the de facto party leader. Even though he is the obvious choice for the Social Liberals, Østergaard will have a hard time filling the shoes of Vestager, who was held in high esteem by the electorate.

New party leader: The Conservative People's Party

Declining electoral support, as indicated both by the EP elections and opinion polls, and internal debate following on from this, made Lars Barfoed resign as Conservative Party chair. On 6 August he announced that he had gotten approval from the executive committee to appoint Søren Pape Poulsen as chair. At the annual party congress in late September this selection was approved by the party membership. Søren Pape Poulsen is an interesting and not ‘classic conservative’ choice since he is not part of the parliamentary group and not well known among the wider public. He was, at the time of his election, the mayor of Viborg, a medium‐sized municipality in Jutland. He is homosexual, and the Conservatives are split on homosexual issues. It remains to be seen whether this bold choice is the electoral remedy that the Conservatives desperately need.

Liberals

Lars Løkke Rasmussen, the Liberal Party chair and former Prime Minister in 2009–2011 survived as party chair and hence the Prime Minister candidate of the opposition after internal debate and a decisive executive committee meeting on 3 June. The fact that the Liberals had paid for clothing to him amounting to the equivalent of €20,000 sparked debate and questioned his position both because Lars Løkke Rasmussen previously has had bad press coverage on the use of public money for private purposes (see Bille 2014: 108) and because spending this amount on clothing is very far from what a large part of the hinterland finds appropriate.

Issues in national politics

An issue that has been high on the agenda all through the electoral period is the unemployment insurance reform decided by the bourgeois government in 2010 together with the Social Liberals (see Bille 2014: 107). There is deep disagreement with the reform among the Social Democrats and two left‐wing parties – a sentiment shared by large parts of the electorate. The Social Democrats have, in the name of government cooperation, had to accept this reform.

Another prominent theme on the political agenda regards asylum and integration policies. In particular, the large number of asylum seekers from Syria put pressure on the Danish system and increased the figures; something the government, in particular the Social Democrats, could not witness without taking action. Hence, the government imposed stricter immigration policies, even though it did not thrill the Social Liberals. The Social Democrats are interested in not appearing soft in order not to lose additional electoral support to the Danish People's Party.

The Finance Act for 2015 was settled with the support of the Red‐Green Alliance and Socialist People's Party. The supporting parties asked for changes in the unemployment insurance scheme, but the government managed to get their support by shrinking the masks of the safety net for those that fall out of the system, but not altering the system as such. The provision of a ‘red’ budget with initiatives on welfare, health and climate increases the chance that the government may regain some of its electoral loses to the centre‐right opposition.

By the end of 2014 unemployment benefit and immigration/integration seem to be two important issues on the agenda for the election year of 2015 as both issues split the current government. In order to regain prime ministerial office, these issues not only have to be settled but more of the government's political proposals need to be completed. They must also hope for better economic indicators in order to catch up with the Liberal‐led government alternative.
