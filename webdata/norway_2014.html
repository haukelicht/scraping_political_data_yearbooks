Introduction

The political year 2014 was a relatively quiet one in Norway. It saw no national or local elections, and the government consisting of the Conservatives and the Progress Party stayed in office without any changes in composition. Popular support for the government did, however, decrease substantially, and the loss in the opinion polls was particularly large for the Progress Party.

Cabinet report

In 2013, the Conservative Party and the Progress Party formed a minority coalition government (Solberg I) (see Bergh & Karlsen 2014). No cabinet changes occurred during 2014.

Parliament report

The composition of parliament in 2014 reflected the 2013 election. The few changes in gender composition that can be observed between 1 January and 31 December are due to three members on long‐term leave of absence (parental leave and long‐term sick leave).

Institutional changes

There were no major institutional changes in the Norwegian government in 2014. The party system also remained unchanged. However, two of the parties from the governing coalition that lost power in the 2013 election – the Labour Party and the Centre Party – changed leadership in 2014. The leader of the Centre Party, Liv Signe Navarsete, resigned in April and was replaced by Trygve Slagsvold Vedum. Former Prime Minister Jens Stoltenberg was appointed Secretary‐General of NATO and was replaced as Labour Party leader by Jonas Gahr Støre in June.

Issues in national politics

During 2014 the fortunes of the newly elected government declined from widespread support early in the year, to widespread critique toward the end of the year. The centre‐right government that came into power after the 2013 parliamentary election did not have a majority in parliament. In order to govern and get its policies through parliament, it relies on the support of the two centrist parties: the Liberal Party and the Christian Democratic Party. Cooperation between the two parties in government – the Conservative Party and the Progress Party – and the two centrist parties is regulated by an agreement that was signed after the 2013 election. The language in the agreement is fairly vague; it provides a general framework for cooperation, and leaves most specific policy decisions to be made at a later point (see Bergh & Karlsen 2014). It was clear from the start that there was a potential for political clashes between the government and its two partners. Policy differences with respect to issues such as immigration, foreign aid and environmental protection are especially large between the right‐wing populist Progress Party, on the one hand, and the centrist parties, on the other.

The government seemed to enjoy a period of good will in the first part of 2014. Public opinion polls indicated that people approved of the new government, and cooperation between the four centre‐right partners worked fairly well. The four parties did, for instance, reach an agreement on immigration and asylum policy in February. There were, however, a few unfortunate incidents in the spring of 2014 that in a sense forewarned of future troubles for the government. Prime Minister Erna Solberg and her government decided, for instance, not to meet with the Dalai Lama when he visited Norway in May. This decision was widely criticised and seen as a sign of weakness and giving in to pressure from China. Numerous politicians outside of the government met with the Dalai Lama, including the leaders of the Liberal and Christian Democratic parties.

Another contentious issue concerned the right of medical doctors (general practitioners) to refuse to refer their patients to hospitals for abortions. The government gave this ‘right of refusal’ to doctors as part of an agreement with the Christian Democratic Party. This resulted in a public opinion backlash and large protests on International Women's Day on 8 March. This in turn led to a modification of the proposal that made it clear that women's access to abortions would not be hampered.

In the autumn of 2014, the government faced serious difficulties over its first budget proposal and over its treatment of children seeking asylum. The budget proposal did not meet with approval from the Liberals and the Christian Democrats. The most contentious issues concerned taxes, foreign aid and what was seen as a less than environmentally friendly budget. The Liberals and Christian Democrats vocally opposed the budget in public and the end‐result was that the government had to retract a number of its proposals. This may have left a perception of a weak government that was beholden to the two smaller centrist parties.

A major issue erupted in December, when it was revealed that the Minister of Justice, Anders Anundsen, seemed not to have followed through on parts of the agreement with the centre parties on asylum policy. In what appeared to be a direct violation of the agreement signed in February, a number of asylum‐seeking children with several years of residence in Norway were expelled from the country. This provoked a strong reaction from the Liberals and the Christian Democrats, and the debate over this issue continued into 2015.

As various splits within the centre‐right coalition became evident in the autumn, the Labour Party surged in the opinion polls, while the governing parties, and especially the Progress Party, lost support. The goodwill that voters expressed towards the government early in the year seemed to have evaporated.
