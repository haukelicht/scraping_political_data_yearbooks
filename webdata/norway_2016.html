Introduction

There being no major elections in Norway in 2016, the main domestic political event was the somewhat tough negotiations over the government's proposed budget in October. The government‐initiated reform of municipal amalgamations led to several local referenda on the issue, most of which resulted in votes against a proposed merger. As Norwegian politics experienced a peaceful year with few exciting events, the news agenda was dominated by international political events.

Election report

There were no national or local elections in Norway in 2016.

Cabinet report

The coalition government of the Conservative Party and Progress Party was formed in October 2013, and for two years, Prime Minister Solberg stuck to her initial team of cabinet ministers. It was not until December 2015 that the first reshuffle took place with a total of seven new appointees, of which two were reassigned from other ministerial positions (see Bergh & Karlsen 2016). In December 2016, three more ministers were replaced. Two male ministers from the Progress Party were replaced with two men from the same party. One woman from the Conservative Party was replaced by a man from the same party. Hence, the number of ministers and the allocation of seats between the parties did not change. However, the level of female representation dropped slightly, from 47 to 42 per cent.

Institutional changes

The only elections held in 2016 were several referenda on municipal amalgamations. The government‐initiated reform met quite a bit of resistance at the local level as voters in many municipalities voted no to the proposal. The cases in which all of the municipalities in a proposed merger voted for the reform were rare. This issue of municipal level reform was kept alive throughout the year, and developments suggest that the scope of the reform will fall short of the initial goals set by the government.

Issues in national politics

The news coverage and public discourse in Norway in 2016 was dominated by international events such as the Brexit referendum in the UK, the attempted coup in Turkey, and naturally, the election of President Trump in the US. When compared to these events, Norwegian political life in 2016 seems even more tranquil than usual.

Probably the largest political event in Norway was the negotiations over the government's proposed budget in October. The sitting coalition government of the Conservative and Progress parties do not have a majority in parliament (Stortinget), so they rely on the support of two small centrist parties (the Liberals and the Christian Democrats) to pass the budget and other measures. The Progress Party (whose leader is the Minister of Finance) got off to a poor start by issuing an ultimatum over gasoline and car taxes. The centrist parties balked at this, and wanted stronger pro‐environment (or anti‐car) policies in the budget. There were several breakdowns of the negotiations, and the fall of the sitting government seemed a real possibility for a while. In the end they reached an agreement, but this was the most difficult of the many problematic budget‐processes for this government.

Among other issues that were major parts of public debate in 2016 were the asylum‐seekers whose place of origin was usually Africa or the Middle East, but had arrived in Norway from over the border with Russia; most were returned to Russia early in the year. The government was criticised by some for not processing individual applications for asylum from this group, and for returning people to an uncertain future in Russia. The trial in which the terrorist Anders Breivik sued the government for inhumane treatment in prison also received a lot of attention, especially since Breivik won the lower level court suit (the ruling was overturned by an appeals court in early 2017).
