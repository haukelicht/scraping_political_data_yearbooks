Cabinet formation

It was not very obvious which parties should govern the Netherlands after the elections of November 2006. In December the option of a coalition between the Christian Democrats (CDA), the Labour Party (PvdA) and the Socialist Party (SP) had been explored and rejected. A more realistic option seemed to be a coalition of CDA, PvdA and the Christian Union. On 20 December, the Queen had appointed an informateur, Herman Wijffels (member of the World Bank Board of Directors and also member of the CDA), to initiate and facilitate negotiations between the three parties. By 7 February 2007, he reached an agreement with the three parties, entitled Working together, living together (Samen werken, samen leven). Sustainable growth, respect and solidarity were defined as primary goals. The government was to strive for a new balance between change and security, while working with citizens as well as social organisations. Compared to its predecessor, it would be less oriented towards economic liberalism and more towards social cohesion and participation. On 9 February, the leader of the CDA and former Prime Minister, Jan Peter Balkenende, was appointed formateur and within two weeks a new cabinet was sworn in by the Queen. It was the first time that the Christian Union joined a government coalition. The Union resulted from a merger (in 2000) between two orthodox Protestant parties that had never participated in a national government.

Election report

The Eerste Kamer (upper chamber or senate) was elected on 29 May by the provincial councillors who had been elected by the people on 7 March 2007. Provincial issues played a modest role in the provincial elections, with many voters following their national party preferences. The results resembled the outcome of the parliamentary elections of 2006: the mainstream parties – CDA, PvdA, VVD and D66 – lost support, whereas the left‐wing Socialist Party (SP), the Animal Rights Party (Partij voor de Dieren, PvdD) and the Christian Union gained it. The GreenLeft (GroenLinks, GL), which had become a fairly moderate green party, did not do very well. If the right‐wing Freedom Party (Partij voor de Vrijheid, PVV) had participated, very likely it would have won quite a few seats at the expense of the Liberal Party (Volkspartij voor Vrijheid en Democratie, VVD). The VVD suffered only minor losses. Some provincial parties also lost seats, whereas others won them; as a result, they continued to be represented in the Eerste Kamer by the Independent Senate Group (Onafhankelijke Senaatsfractie, OSF). The List Pim Fortuyn (LPF), which had lost its seats in every provincial council, also lost its seat in the Eerste Kamer. In August, the party decided to dissolve. In spite of the losses of CDA and PvdA, the coalition maintained a narrow majority in the senate, owing to the gains of the Christian Union.

As usual, the candidates for the senate had been selected by their parties – whether by a party congress or by a membership vote – and ranked in order of preference. However, some provincial councillors refused to follow party instructions and voted for another candidate on the list – often someone from their own region. Five senators were elected in spite of being on the list that was considered ‘inelegible’. In one case, this led to a party split. Düzgün Yildirim, a Socialist candidate of Turkish origin, refused to give up his senate seat at the request of the SP leadership. In September he was expelled from the party, and a month later he founded a new party, with the name Solidara.

Issues in national politics

Rita Verdonk had been a controversial Minister for Foreigners and Integration in the cabinets of Balkenende II and III. In 2006 she ran for the leadership of the VVD, but was defeated (by a narrow margin) by Mark Rutte (see Lucardie 2007: 1045, 1047). Yet in the parliamentary election she won more preferential votes than Rutte, which was a unique event in Dutch electoral history: the head of the party list always obtains more votes than any other candidate. Tensions between the two politicians flared up frequently in 2007. After Verdonk had been quoted in a national daily criticising the leadership of Rutte in September, she was expelled from the parliamentary group (fractie). In October she decided to leave the party (while maintaining her seat in parliament) in order to found a new political movement, ‘Proud of the Netherlands' (‘Trots op Nederland’). Polls predicted (by the end of October) 19 seats for the new movement, whereas the VVD would obtain only 12. At the end of the year, the popularity of Trots op Nederland had declined a little (13 seats in the polls).

Institutional changes

There were no changes in 2007.
