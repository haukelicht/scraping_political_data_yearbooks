Introduction

In 2014 Portugal celebrated the fortieth anniversary of the Revolution of Carnations. The most important event of the year was that the Portuguese government was able to complete the programme of austerity measures with the Troika in May. Moreover, the European Parliament elections were a major test for the government, which was punished by the electorate for its austerity policies.

Election report

European Parliament elections

The campaign was dominated by national, but also European, politics related to the financial crisis. The austerity measures of the government were the major aspect of contestation by the opposition parties. While the government wanted to complete the programme and avoid further bail‐outs, the three main opposition parties wanted a re‐negotiation of the bail‐out. A major cleavage among the parliamentary parties was that of support or rejection of the European Union.

In terms of results, the Socialists won the elections with eight seats. In spite of the collapse of the coalition government vote to 28 per cent (and seven seats), the victory of the Socialists was rather meagre, strengthening the general opposition to the secretary general José Seguro. Among the five parliamentary parties, the big winner was the Communist‐Green coalition with three seats. They were able to profit considerably from the opposition to the austerity policies of the government. In contrast, the result of the Bloc Left was rather poor and below expectations with only 5 per cent and one seat.

A total of 12 other small parties took part in the elections. Just one of them was able to achieve representation: the Party of Earth (Partido da Terra, PT), led by António Marinho e Pinto, who was a former president of the Portuguese lawyers’ association. His party got 7 per cent of votes and two EP seats. The well‐known former PSD MP Rui Tavares created a new party called Livre (Free). Although it did not achieve representation, it won 2 per cent of votes on a small budget of €25,000.

Cabinet report

After weeks of speculation, Prime Minister Coelho decided to introduce a minor reshuffle of his cabinet on 20 November. He replaced the Home Office Minister Miguel Macedo, who had been part of the government since its beginning in 2011, with law professor Anabela Miranda Rodrigues. She had been director of the Centre of Judicial Studies between 2004 and 2009, which is the main training institution for judges. She became the first woman to take over the Home Office Ministry. The main reason for the replacement of Macedo was the alleged scandal around the gold visa programe to financially rich foreigners that may contribute to the economy of the country. Macedo decided to resign in order to protect the government.

Parliamentary report

Commemorating the 40th anniversary of the Revolution of Carnations

Due to the austerity measures of the government, the parliamentary and extra‐parliamentary opposition were quite divided about the main place to celebrate the 40th anniversary of the Revolution of Carnations on 25 April. The extra‐parliamentary opposition came mainly from the association 25th of April, representing the military officers that staged the successful coup d’état and were prominent during the revolutionary process. After negotiations between the president of the assembly of the republic and the president of the association, Vasco Lourenço, no joint celebration in the parliament was agreed, and the military veterans decided to organise a separate commemoration in another part of Lisbon. This clearly showed that even the 25 April and the developments afterwards remained a major dividing line between the centre‐right coalition and the parliamentary and extra‐parliamentary left. The austerity measures of the government further exacerbated this divide.

The government budget for 2015

The centre‐right coalition government presented the last budget of the legislative period, and the first post‐Troika. The budget showed continuity of previous austerity policies, but now with softer initiatives. Most of the austerity measures of previous years remained in place, but no new ones were introduced. An increase in tax for beer and alcoholic drinks was included in the budget. Benefits for some pensioners were cut slightly, particularly those with higher pensions. Overall the tax figures were quite optimistic because the level of expenditure decreased faster than revenue.

In the final vote, the coalition government had its budget approved against the opposition parties. Additionally, four Social Democratic MPs representing Madeira voted against the budget. There was one abstention from a member of the CDS‐PP Madeira. The main reason for the opposition of the PSD Madeira was that a transfer of €70 million to the Madeira government was not introduced in the budget.

Institutional changes

After general criticisms of the leadership of José Seguro, the secretary‐general of the Socialist Party, primaries were set up for 28 September. In these primaries, António Costa, the mayor of Lisbon, won a resounding victory among the members of the party. António Costa got 69.6 per cent and José Seguro 29.9 per cent. The new leader is quite charismatic and advocated a major change towards a better social approach to the austerity programme of the Troika and government. He clearly is very keen to be seen as reliable in relation to the European Union. In November, he was confirmed victor at the 20th Socialist Party conference with 96 per cent approval. The conference was designed to project unity around the new leader to the outside world.

Issues in national politics

The Troika leaves the country

After three years of a heavy programme of austerity measures agreed with the Troika, the Portuguese centre‐right coalition government saw light at the end of the tunnel. After the positive exit of Ireland from the agreed Troika government programme, the Portuguese government was quite optimistic that also Portugal would also be able to complete the programme in May as scheduled originally, and without needing a cautionary second programme. The official 2013 figures for the budget deficit were quite encouraging. Due to increased unexpected revenue, the final figure was 4.9 per cent, down from 6.2 percent of the previous year, but below the 5.2 per cent agreed with the Troika. The other side of the story was the growing public debt of the Portuguese government, which by the end of 2013 had risen to a quite worrying 129 per cent. Although unemployment went slightly down throughout 2014, it was still 13.8 per cent at the end of the year. The final report of the Troika was in May 2014, following which the Portuguese government was able to govern more freely and without the three‐monthly Troika monitoring missions.

Judicial reform and the new judicial map

Judicial reform has been ongoing for some years. It started during the previous Socialist governments and was continued by Coelho's coalition government. The newly reformed judiciary system began in September. The basic idea of the reform was to make the judicial sector more efficient in the long run. Apart from the amalgamation of courts and reduction of judiciary districts, the reform was intended to modernise the way trials are run. Moreover, the reduction of the highly legalistic written culture of the court system towards a more informal one was also part of the reform. One major criticism was that the time span was far too short to achieve a proper change to the new structure. In addition, the whole reform was underfunded, mainly due to the austerity policies of the government. At the end of the year, many new amalgamated courts were installed in provisional accommodation with poor work conditions.

A misconceived policy of investment attraction: The golden visas scandal

In 2013, Vice‐Minister Paulo Portas introduced a new scheme for investment in Portugal. The difficulties in getting investment into the country during the implementation of the programme of austerity measures led to the creation of a new scheme called ‘Residence Permit for Investment Activities’, or more popularly known as the ‘golden visas’. The visa would allow incumbents to have access to the Schengen space as well. Potential investors had to show that they had no criminal record and had no negative register in the Portuguese Foreigners and Borders Service, or in the information system of Schengen. According to Europol, however, the golden visas scheme facilitated money laundering – a claim refuted by Portas.

Allegedly, throughout 2014, some high‐ranking officials under the supervision of the Home Office, and also persons close to Minister Miguel Macedo, were investigated about corruption in the awarding of golden visas. The judicial police arrested 11 persons suspected of corruption, money laundering, traffic of influences and embezzlement. This resulted in the resignation of Macedo, although he was not charged with any offence. The majority of golden visas are requested from Chinese citizens who want to buy property. In total, about €100 million per month was invested in the country through this scheme. However, 90 per cent of the investment is directed towards the housing sector.

The collapse of the Espirito Santo Bank

Probably the most important bank in Portugal was the Banco Espirito Santo (BES), with strong connections to major political figures such as former President of the European Commission José Manuel Barroso. The incumbent president of the bank was Ricardo Salgado, one of the members of the Espirito Santo family. Although he had been quite successful between 2002 and 2008, many management mistakes and inadequate expansion of business operations led to the collapse of the bank at the end of July and early August. Shortly before its outright collapse, the Bank of Portugal intervened in order to protect the interests of the deposit holders. In the last years, it became known that BES was selling investment products to deposit holders that were illicit and were used to finance the debt‐ridden enterprises linked to the larger Espirito Santo holding (Grupo Espirito Santo, GES). Salgado was warned several times by the Bank of Portugal to resolve the excessive debt burden of the BES. Moreover, BES had also to comply with the official 9 per cent threshold of liquidity required from all the banks after the financial reforms introduced by the European Union. BES failed to achieve this.

The intervention of the Bank of Portugal on 31 July was a major event in Portuguese banking history. Throughout the last week of July, the price of BES shares in the Portuguese stock exchange collapsed to 20 cents. The Bank of Portugal, in cooperation with the European Central Bank, agreed that the BES would cease to exist and split it into a bad and good bank. The debt‐ridden investment part of the bank would be transferred to a bad bank called ‘BES’, while the deposit holders would belong to a ‘New Bank’ (Novo Banco). Although the Troika had left the country, €12 billion of the bail‐out had been reserved for the recapitalisation of the banks. About €6 billion was still available and therefore the government could have funded the losses of €3.5 billion, but probably not the additional estimated € 4.7 billion in losses of the larger GES holding. Prime Minister Coelho was against an intervention by the government, so a private solution was found which required the other banks to foot the bill for part of the bail‐out of the BES. The so‐called ‘Fund of Banking Resolution’, to which all other national banks belonged, was asked to contribute with €4.9 billion, while the government would lend €3.9 billion from the Troika bail‐out money. The Fund of Banking Resolution would receive the Novo Banco as guarantee. At the end of the year, the first phase towards the sale of the Novo Banco was completed, with several national banks showing interest in acquiring it. However, the decision related to the debt‐ridden part of the BES was contested by many investors, including the American investment bank Goldman Sachs. The latter owned 2 per cent of BES (Ferreira 2014a, 2014b; Babo & Gago 2014).

Arrest of former Prime Minister José Socrates

At the end of the year, José Socrates, who had been prime minister from 2005 to 2011, was arrested at Lisbon airport. He was kept in preventive custody for the rest of the year without charge. The main reason for his preventive detention was to clarify if the former prime minister was involved in corruption, money laundering and tax evasion. In spite of several habeas corpus submissions to the Supreme Court by citizens supporting Socrates, he was not released. Some figureheads in the opposition, such as eminence grise Mário Soares visited him in prison in Èvora and denounced preventive detention without charge as a politically motivated act.
