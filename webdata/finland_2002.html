Election report

The 2003 election was the most dramatic in years in Finland. The Lipponen Rainbow Government had ruled the country since the 1995 election, and prolonged its mandate at the 1999 election. Most of the established parties from left to right, including the Greens, were included in these two cabinets, leaving aside the major political actor in Finnish politics: the Centre Party. Red Earth cabinet coalitions had dominated the political arena for decades. Red Earth was a compromise between the Agrarian Centre Party and the Social Democrats dating back to 1937 that protected Finland from extreme radicalism on both the left and right. In the 2003 election much was at stake for the Centre Party, as a third period in opposition could be disastrous for the party. As a result, much of the campaign was focused on the coming cabinet formation. Other issues were the unemployment rate, which the former cabinet had failed to resolve, and a debate around Prime Minster Lipponen's meeting with President Bush before the Iraq invasion.

The 2003 election has been called the ‘prime minister election’ as the two main prime ministerial candidates and party leaders – Paavo Lipponen for the Social Democrats and Anneli Jäätteenmäki for the Centre Party – competed in the Helsinki constituency. Ville Itälä, who was the National Coalition party leader, had a similar ambition, but failed in the shadow of these two main competitors. The Centre Party went to the election with their new female party leader, which gave a lot of positive attention to the party. One of Anneli Jäätteenmäki's main attacks on Lipponen was focused on his discussion with Bush about Finnish involvement in the Iraq War. During the last weeks of the campaign, she referred to documents that showed promises of support from Lipponen. The voters were left in ignorance as to whether she or Lipponen, who denied the claim, was right or wrong. This debate left a deep rift between the two main competitors.

The election was a success for the Centre Party in both votes and seats. The Centre Party managed to become the biggest party, followed by the Social Democrats, who made small progress in terms of seats and votes. The big loser was the conservative National Coalition. During the campaign, the National Coalition leader, Ville Itälä, had the role of spectator while Lipponen and Jäätteenmäki debated the Iraq issue and the coming cabinet formation.

Issues in politics

After the election, the leader of the biggest party, Anneli Jäätteenmäki, was given the role of forming a cabinet. In the beginning of the negotiations, the Greens were left out and later on the National Coalition followed. The cabinet was formed around the Red Earth axis composed of the Centre Party and the Social Democrats with eight seats each and Jäätteenmäki as Prime Minister. Paavo Lipponen would not accept being a minister under Jäätteenmäki, so he was chosen to be chairman of the newly elected parliament. A coalition with these two parties would have been enough for a majority cabinet, but in a surprise move, the Swedish People's Party was included with two seats. The party had lost three seats and saw their inclusion as a lotto victory.

In the beginning, the cabinet had a good start and Finland was led by a female president and a female prime minister. However, during the campaign, Jäätteenmäki had referred to documents on the Bush and Lipponen meeting without referring to their source. She denied having access to any secret documents, but it soon leaked out to the press that she probably had received information from the Foreign Ministry. The police had already begun investigating the case. On 18 June, she had to explain to Parliament about the secret information. At that moment, more information about the affair was known, but she continued to declare her innocence. Parliament lost its confidence in her and the Social Democrats made it clear that the same cabinet composition could continue, but the prime minster would have to be changed. As a result, Jäätteenmäki resigned both the premiership and the party leadership. Few politicians defended her, even among her own ranks. While the documents could in principle be open to opposition leaders, she denied their existence instead of claiming her right to have access to them. Similar procedures were routine in the past, but now it got huge media attention. Nor was the content of the documents controversial. President Bush complimented Lipponen in the document and welcomed Finland as a coalition partner in the war against Iraq, which she used in her campaign, although in fact Lipponen had promised only to give humanitarian aid after the war. This could be seen as an indication that Bush and Lipponen had different views of their common agreement. Speculations have been made whether this was an act of gender discrimination or not. She was later prosecuted, but found not guilty of any criminal act.

Matti Vanhanen, son of the professor of political science Tatu Vanhanen, was chosen to as the new Centre Party leader and Prime Minster for the new cabinet. During his first months as Prime Minister, he, as a teetotaller, had to accept an amendment reducing the alcohol tax. Finns are known to drink heavily and behave violently, and when the Estonians join the European Union in May 2004 cheap alcohol was expected to be carried into the country. According to the Government, tax reduction will protect Finland from that threat. Many voices were raised against the amendment, as alcohol consumption is expected to increase, resulting in severe social problems.

A new language act was accepted obliging public authorities and publicly controlled companies to provide equal language rights in practice to the Swedish population. The Sami population also got guarantees of public service in their native language. In addition, a law was enacted that reduces discrimination due to age, handicap, belief or sexual orientation.
