Introduction

The political year 2014 was dominated by European and parliamentary elections, a change of government and a serious crisis for the new government shortly after taking office. Above all, events in 2014 highlighted what seems to be a new era of instability in Swedish politics.

Election report

European Parliament elections, May 2014

Sweden's fifth election to the EP was held on Sunday, 25 May. Turnout surpassed the 50 per cent mark for the first time at an EU election. The Social Democrats and the Moderates lost one seat each, as did the People's Party Liberals. For the Centre and Christian Democratic parties, however, there was relief after both had performed poorly in polls after the 2010 parliamentary election.

The main winners were the Sweden Democrats, who with 9.7 per cent of the vote and two seats entered the EP for the first time. There was a notable result also for the Feminist Initiative (FI), with 5.5 per cent and one seat – its first electoral breakthrough. As newcomers in the EP, FI joined the Social Democrats in the Socialists and Democrats (S&D) group. The Sweden Democrats had been courted by the French Front National in an attempt to form the European Alliance for Freedom (EAF), but instead joined Europe For Freedom and Democracy (EFD), which was shortly afterwards renamed ‘Europe For Freedom and Direct Democracy’ (EFDD). It appears that SD's two MEPs had to make a public announcement disowning their party's history before they were allowed into EFDD (Watt 2014).

Parliamentary, regional and local elections, September 2014

The parliamentary election held on 14 September was caused by the natural end of the four‐year parliamentary term. As has been the case since 1970, regional and local elections were held on the same date, and in some cases together with subnational referendums. The four‐party centre‐right Alliance, in office since 2006, was behind in the polls when the campaign started. The main strategy of the Alliance parties was to emphasise stability and portray the red‐green (Social Democrats, Greens and Left Party) opposition as divided and economically irresponsible. It was nevertheless a difficult campaign for the government, having to defend a record of increased unemployment after two terms in office.

Other campaign issues included welfare and immigration. The Left Party demanded a full stop to all private profit‐making in the health and welfare sectors. Knowing that close cooperation with the Left Party had been an electoral liability in 2010, Social Democrat leader Stefan Löfven was not willing to make such a commitment, but the Alliance parties repeatedly argued that a government led by Löfven would have to rely on the Left Party one way or another. Unlike 2010, however, there was no joint red‐green manifesto. Immigration was put on the agenda on 16 August when Prime Minister (and Moderate leader) Fredrik Reinfeldt stated that increasing numbers of asylum seekers would bring extra costs, which Sweden ought to carry. Some commentators suggested that the portrayal of immigration as an economic burden played into the hands of the Sweden Democrats (Dagens Nyheter 2014a).

Immigration is the main profile issue of the Sweden Democrats and it showed in their campaign. The party's meetings received a lot of attention, partly due the noisy, but for the most part non‐violent, counter‐demonstrations. Opinion polls had indicated a lead for the opposition throughout 2013 and 2014, and although the lead dwindled as the election drew closer, a change of government seemed the most likely outcome. The Alliance parties had declared that they would resign, and not try to block the formation of a red‐green government, if the Alliance ended up with fewer seats than the red‐green parties put together.

This is what happened. The red‐green bloc received a total of 159 seats against the Alliance parties’ 141. This was 16 seats short of the 175 required for an outright majority, and the combined net gain of three seats for the three red‐green parties must be regarded as a disappointment after eight years of a Moderate‐led government with welfare cuts and increased unemployment. Not least for the Greens, who for long had seemed certain to improve on their 2010 result, but ended up with an unchanged number of seats. For the Social Democrats it was a marginal improvement on 2010, but still the party's second worst performance in a parliamentary election since the establishment of universal suffrage. There were losses for all four Alliance parties, especially the Moderates, but the result was a relief for the Centre Party and the Christian Democrats, who had both been near or below the 4 per cent representational threshold in several polls.

The only real winner was the Sweden Democrats, who more than doubled their share of the vote and emerged as the third biggest party with almost twice as many seats as the Greens in fourth place. The result confirmed the transformation of a party, which for many years had been regarded as an extremist fringe phenomenon, into an established political force. It remained ostracised by all other parties, and the party's success meant that it would be even more difficult than in 2010–2014 to form a majority government. The FI was unable to repeat its success from the EP election, but came within less than one percentage point of the 4 per cent threshold. Turnout was 85.8 per cent, up 1.2 points from 2010. For more on the 2014 parliamentary election, see Aylott and Bolin (2015).

Cabinet report

Reinfeldt II cabinet, ending 3 October 2014

The last nine months of the Alliance government went without personnel changes or major events. On the whole, the government was, throughout its eight‐year tenure, able to maintain a much higher level of cohesion than previous centre‐right governments. Opinion polls indicated that Reinfeldt remained the country's most popular party leader, although his lead over Löfven tended to be quite narrow. The pair of Reinfeldt and Finance Minister Anders Borg remained a key asset for the government, but if their personal appeal had been a major factor in 2006 and 2010, it could not prevent defeat in 2014.

Löfven I cabinet, beginning 3 October 2014

Reinfeldt announced the resignation of his government on election night. As leader of the biggest opposition party, Stefan Löfven was asked by the Speaker of Parliament to try and form a new government. Löfven announced that he did not want the Left Party included in a coalition, and expressed interest in some form of agreement with one or more of the Alliance parties, but this was turned down immediately. Government negotiations between the Social Democrats and the Greens ensued. Not all potentially divisive issues, such as energy, could be resolved fully, but an agreement was reached just over two weeks later. Löfven was elected prime minister in a vote of investiture on Thursday, 2 October. The two governing parties commanded only 138 parliamentary seats, but with only the Sweden Democrats voting against, Löfven was elected with a comfortable margin. According to the Swedish constitution, a government or minister is regarded as tolerated as long as a majority of all MPs do not vote against, meaning that abstentions and absences are de facto passive support for the government.

Consisting of 18 Social Democrats and six Greens, the members of Löfven's first cabinet were announced on 3 October. It was a comparatively youthful cabinet with an average age of 45.6 compared to 50.6 at the end of the Reinfeldt cabinet. The 27 year‐old Minister for Secondary and Adult Education, Aida Hadzialic, became Sweden's youngest‐ever minister. There was a lack of previous government experience in the new cabinet. Only four of the 24 ministers had previously served in cabinet; ten had no government or parliamentary experience (including Löfven and Finance Minister Magdalena Andersson). In cabinet for the first time, the Green ministers included the party's two spokespersons since 2011: Åsa Romson and Gustav Fridolin.

Parliamentary report

The 2014 election result meant that the pivotal position of SD between the two main blocs was reinforced. No other party was willing to cooperate with SD, meaning that cross‐bloc agreements were the only way to prevent SD from becoming permanent veto players. Although Löfven left the door open for negotiations with the Alliance parties, they showed little interest. This meant that the future of Löfven's government was uncertain. An early major test was the budget. A first step towards an agreement with the Left Party was taken on 6 October when a deal was reached about private welfare profits. However, the Alliance parties declared that they would support their own joint budget proposal, prepared while still in government. The budget decision thus depended on the Sweden Democrats. On 2 December, the evening before the budget vote, SD declared that they would support the Alliance budget proposal in the decisive vote. The party also stated that it would vote down all government budgets until the other parties were prepared to negotiate with them.

In the chamber on 3 December, the Alliance budget was carried with support from SD. Löfven's initial response was to renew the negotiation invitation to the Alliance parties. When this was rejected, the Prime Minister had two main options. He and his government could resign, in which case the Speaker of Parliament would initiate a process to find a new government. He could also decide to stay in office but dissolve parliament and call a new election. Löfven opted for the latter, envisaging an election on 22 March 2015. The formal announcement was delayed, however, as a newly elected parliament cannot be dissolved until three months after its first meeting (i.e., 28 December). Premature dissolutions of the Swedish parliament are rare, having taken place most recently in 1958. This is mainly because an election caused by premature dissolution does not start a new four‐year parliamentary term. The new parliament instead continues the term begun by the parliament elected in the most recent ordinary election.

Secret meetings between the Alliance and the government began in mid‐December, culminating on 27 December with the ‘December Agreement’. Set to operate until 2022, the Agreement in essence meant that the smaller of the two main blocs would not prevent a minority government from getting its budget through parliament. This deprived the Sweden Democrats of their veto power and removed the need to dissolve parliament before the next ordinary election. At the same time, the Agreement did not protect the government from defeat on all issues, as it did not cover areas such as education and migration. The Agreement thus stabilised the situation in the short term, but its longer‐term sustainability was uncertain. It also meant that the red‐green government would have to administer an Alliance budget until changes could be decided in 2015.

Institutional changes

There were no leadership changes in the major political parties in 2014, but the Moderate Party was set for a leadership change in 2015. On election night, Fredrik Reinfeldt announced his resignation as party leader. The following day, outgoing Finance Minister Anders Borg also announced his retirement from politics. Reinfeldt was not to formally step down until 2015, but in practice abandoned the political frontline shortly after the new parliament had opened. Anna Kinberg Batra, head of the Moderate parliamentary party since 2010 and appointed economics spokesperson on Borg's retirement, soon emerged as the de facto new leader and firm favourite to take over the formal leadership in 2015. Sweden Democrats leader Jimmie Åkesson went on sick leave on 17 October. The reason given was exhaustion, although he also stated that attacks on him and his party had been a contributory factor (Dagens Nyheter 2014b). He remained on sick leave for the remainder of the year. It was uncertain when he would return. Mattias Karlsson, who had replaced Björn Söder as head of the SD parliamentary party after the election, took over as acting leader.

Issues in national politics

Gross domestic product (GDP) grew by 2.1 per cent in 2014 – a marginal increase compared to the previous year – according to the National Institute of Economic Research (NIER). Inflation continued to fluctuate around, often below, zero according to Statistics Sweden. The central bank Riksbanken lowered the repo (repurchase agreement) interest rate twice; in late October, it went down to 0 per cent. According to the National Debt Office, there was a fiscal deficit of SEK72 billion (€8 billion), SEK61 billion less than in 2013. National debt grew to SEK1.394 billion (€155 billion), an increase of SEK 117 billion compared to 2012. Relative to GDP it grew by 1 percentage point to 36 per cent. The overall rate of unemployment, as a percentage of the population aged 16–64, was 6.3 per cent over the year (3.4 per cent in open unemployment and 2.9 per cent in labour market schemes), a decline of 0.4 points compared to 2013 (unemployment figures are from the Swedish Public Employment Service).
