Introduction

The year 2014 was one of turbulence, followed by huge reform plans with few, if any, results. Ministers resigned, parties left government, a new cabinet was formed and the Greens marched out of government. The planned municipal amalgamation ran out after a storm of protest, and so did the planned social and health reforms. All these shortcomings were good news for the opposition awaiting the coming election in 2015.

Election report

The only visible demarcation between parties during the campaign for the EP elections was between the EU‐critical True Finns and the other parties – all of which are pro‐EU. The most pro‐EU party in the eyes of the True Finns is National Coalition and they were accused of being federalist. Prime Minister Katainen reacted by underlying how voters have to choose between responsibility and populism. The True Finns have softened their criticisms of the EU during their last period in opposition. They now neither propose to quit the EU nor the eurozone.

Differences between other parties are hard to recognise. The Social Democrats are positive about the EU but they have to cope with scepticism within their own ranks. The former party chairman Jutta Urpilainen followed a strict policy against a Greece mortgage release, whereas the Left Alliance focused on solidarity with the crisis countries in the south. A split among Centre Party candidates occurred, as the old veteran Paavo Väyrynen has a strong anti‐EU stance in contrast to Olli Rehn, who as a European Commissioner for Economic and Monetary Affairs stands for deeper integration.

Voting turnout remained at a low level. Slightly below 40 per cent voted as compared to national parliament elections where turnout has stalled around 70 per cent. EU‐related themes were much discussed during the campaign, but did not have a mobilising effect. The True Finns increased their strength to 12.9 per cent of the vote, but much less than in the national parliament elections. The Social Democrats lost more votes than any other party but managed to keep their two seats. Changes were primarily small; the Christian Democrats lost their only seat and the Left Alliance was back again after a five‐year absence.

Many well‐known politicians were candidates in the election. Minister Alexander Stubb won the most votes, but chose to stay in Finland and was offered the prime ministership to replace Jyrki Katainen, who was appointed to the European Commission. Two other ministers from Katainen's cabinet were elected: Henna Virkkunen and Merja Kyllönen. Several MPs and former ministers were elected, such as Anneli Jäätteenmäki, Paavo Väyrynen, Olli Rehn (former EU Commissioner) Sirpa Pietikäinen, Heidi Hautala and Miapetra Kumpula‐Natri. The True Finn hardliner Jussi Halla‐aho was one of the top winners of the vote. He holds a strong critical view of the EU, combined with xenophobia. During the campaign and even after the election it was totally unclear which European parliamentary group the True Finns will join. The party ended up in the European Conservatives and Reformists Group, which is somewhat surprising for those who are familiar with Halla‐aho's political view.

Cabinet report

The year 2014 was a turbulent one. Ministers resigned, parties left and a new cabinet was formed. The turbulence began when Katainen's cabinet presented the budget proposal for 2015. Several tax increases were envisaged, along with reductions in pensions, public support to students and child allowances. Defending the poor, the Left Alliance (VAS) could not accept reductions in child allowances and other cuts in welfare. As no compromise was found, the two VAS ministers Paavo Arhinmäki and Mejra Kyllönen marched out in the middle of a cabinet session. These ministers were not replaced in cabinet. Instead, their portfolios were transferred to National Coalition and Social democratic ministers.

The SDP party congress was assembled in early May to elect a chairman. Minister of Finance and SDP party chairman Jutta Urpilainen sought another term, but with the SDP suffering losses in one election after another, she was challenged by Antti Rinne. Rinne promised to give more time to the party, accusing Urpilainen of being too focused on her ministerial role. While Urpilainen faced criticism for her unpopular cuts in social welfare, it is commonly known that there is no room for more public spending in times of increasing budget deficit. She was popular in cabinet and was respected in the EU for her stance on the south European economic crisis. In spite of this, the party congress elected Antti Rinne with 257 votes, against 243 votes for Urpilainen. Initially, the new chairman hesitated whether to take over the Finance Ministry, which Urpilainen had promised to leave if not elected. It took a month for Rinne to finally take a stand and agree to replace Urpilainen.

While Rinne led his campaign against Urpilainen, Prime Minister Jyrki Katainen announced in early April that he would resign as party chairman and prime minister, and would not stand as a candidate in the EP elections and the national parliament elections due in 2015. Consequently, the party congress of the National Coalition assembled in June to elect a new chairman who would also be the new prime minister. Alexander Stubb was elected with 500 votes, defeating his opponent Paula Risikko, with 349 votes. Stubb had a long career in the EP and had served as Minister of Foreign Affairs and Minister of European Affairs and Foreign Trade in Finland.

The new cabinet had not spent much time in office before a deep cleavage between the Greens and the rest of cabinet became apparent. The planned building of a nuclear plant in northern Finland by the Russian firm Rosatom met heavy resistance from the two Green ministers. Chairman Ville Niinistö was interviewed in the Financial Times, where he accused Finland of Finlandisation by giving Russia the power they eagerly strive for in the West. He was strongly criticised by veteran politicians for being non‐patriotic. The cabinet voted with ten ‘yes’ votes and seven ‘no’. Four of SDP ministers, one Swedish People's party minister and the two Green ministers all voted against the proposal in cabinet, although it was backed by a majority of ministers. Directly after the vote, Niinistö pulled the Green ministers out of cabinet. They were replaced by Sirpa Paatero (SDP), Minister of International Development and by Sanni Grahn‐Laasonen (KOK), Minister of Environment.

Parliament report

The Centre Party strengthened its position as the leading opposition party. The True Finns produced much noise, but it was the Centre Party who was in command, thanks to the government which was unable to deliver promised economic reforms. During the year, the two opposition parties were joined by the Left Alliance and the Greens, who left the cabinet in disappointment. As the cabinet struggled on, the opposition waited for the coming election in April 2015. Polls showed 26 per cent support for the Centre Party. The cabinet did the job for them, and party leader Juha Sipilä kept a low profile and waited to take revenge for the disastrous 2011 election.

Institutional changes

Prime Minister Jyrki Katainen was promised a top job in the EU. He took a chance and announced his resignation long before the EP election. His cabinet was by no means a success story. Almost simultaneously, Minister of Finance Jutta Urpilainen stepped down after she was defeated by Antti Rinne, who was elected new party leader.

Issues in national politics

Stubb's government submitted a statement to parliament proclaiming that it would continue Prime Minister Katainen's detailed programme. According to the declaration, the cabinet will boost growth and employment. However, in the budget proposition for 2015 figures showed an increasing budget deficit of €4.5 billion. More loans will be taken, estimating that the state mortgage will increase to €102 billion in 2015.

Finland was one of the countries who strongly criticised the debt crisis in Greece. While Finland was a good example to Greece in how to handle debt in the 1990s, it is no longer a good example. To cope with the problem, the government has put much effort into implementing reforms written in the cabinet programme. Structural reforms have been on the agenda since 2011 when Katainen's cabinet launched its programme. Municipal amalgamations and a reform of health and social care to large units are major parts of the structural reforms. Neither of the reforms has come to a solution. Minister of Transport and Local Government Henna Virkkunen was the key minister behind the municipal reform, urging a mandatory amalgamation. Her vision was that large units are more efficient and money could be saved. However, there was little evidence to back up her stance. Much energy was wasted in the effort, followed by uncertainty and confusion in the municipalities. The reform stalled and Henna Virkkunen took the consequences. She was elected to the EP and left cabinet.

Reforms of health care and social welfare halted as well. The much criticised reform plan exhausted municipal politicians and staff members and left them in total uncertainty. These reforms were prime targets for the opposition, whose support grew in the polls. At the end of the year, all the signs were that the Centre Party will win the April 2015 national parliament election.

One problem with the social and health care reform was that regional administration would get a fourth pillar, where all administrative regions would follow different geographical borders. None of them have directly elected councils. The unitary county system established in 1634 was split into three different regions from 2010, intending to be more efficient. A fourth pillar without direct elected councils was an obstacle much discussed, and the whole system with different pillars was challenged. Even the President of Finland, Sauli Niinistö, delivered sharp critics against the former and the sitting governments. He accused the government of indecision and for changing stands in the structural reform process.

When the EU discussed new sanctions against Russia, Finland allied with those nations not willing to damage the Russian economy. It was a move with self‐interest as trade with Russia is important for business. The plan to build a nuclear plant by Rosatom is an example where Finland stretches the limits given by the EU. In addition, the Russian‐owned shipyard in Helsinki builds icebreakers to be used by the Russian fleet in the Arctic. Tourism from Russia has vanished, with the falling rouble having severe implications for Finnish retailers and hotels. More importantly, Finland does not want to challenge its longstanding friendship with Russia.
